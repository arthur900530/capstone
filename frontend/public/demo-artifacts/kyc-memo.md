# KYC Memo — Alphabet Inc.

**Review type:** OSINT-driven public-source due diligence
**Date:** 2026-05-06
**Scope note:** This memo is based on free/public sources reviewed during this session, including GLEIF, SEC filings, U.S. government sources, UN sanctions data, and major news reporting. It is not a substitute for commercial screening/database checks.

## Executive Summary

Alphabet Inc. appears to be a **high-transparency, publicly listed U.S. issuer** with strong disclosure quality, but it carries **material regulatory and litigation risk** driven by antitrust, privacy, and platform-governance matters.

Key findings:

- **GLEIF:** Alphabet Inc. is listed in GLEIF under **LEI 5493006MHB84DD0ZWV18**, status **ISSUED**, with fully corroborated entity data.
- **Ownership/control:** Although publicly traded, control is concentrated through Alphabet’s dual-class structure. Based on the 2026 proxy, **Larry Page** holds **27.4%** of total voting power and **Sergey Brin** holds **25.3%**; combined, they retain **effective majority voting control (~52.7%)**.
- **Sanctions:** No exact public-source sanctions hits were identified for **Alphabet Inc.**, **Google LLC**, **Larry Page**, **Sergey Brin**, **Sundar Pichai**, or **Roger W. Ferguson Jr.** on the OFAC SDN list or UN consolidated list.
- **PEP:** Public-source PEP exposure is limited, but **Roger W. Ferguson Jr.** should be treated as a **former PEP / PEP-adjacent** case because Alphabet’s proxy states he served as **Vice Chairman of the U.S. Federal Reserve Board**.
- **Litigation/regulatory:** Alphabet/Google has **significant ongoing legal and regulatory exposure**, including U.S. antitrust findings, long-running EU competition cases, and a large Texas privacy settlement.
- **Adverse media:** Adverse media is substantial and recurring, but it is concentrated in **competition, privacy, AI governance, and platform conduct**, not in bribery, corruption, fraud, or direct sanctions-evasion themes.

**Recommendation:** **Approve with EDD**

## Ownership & Control

### Structure

Alphabet uses a **multi-class share structure**:

- **Class A**: 1 vote per share
- **Class B**: 10 votes per share
- **Class C**: non-voting

This structure materially concentrates control in holders of Class B stock.

### Control persons / dominant voting holders

Per Alphabet’s 2026 proxy statement (record date 2026-04-06):

- **Larry Page**: **389,051,160 Class B shares**; **27.4% total voting power**
- **Sergey Brin**: **358,939,978 Class B shares** plus **37,469 Class A shares**; **25.3% total voting power**

**Assessment:** Page and Brin together hold **effective voting control** of Alphabet despite public listing. For KYC/control purposes, they should be treated as the key control persons.

### Other significant holders

- **BlackRock, Inc.**: reported **356,934,964 Class A shares**, about **6.1% of Class A** and **2.5% total voting power**
- **Vanguard**: historical filings reflected roughly **8.5% of Class A** and **3.5% voting power**, but Alphabet’s 2026 proxy notes Vanguard restructured reporting and current subsidiary-level holdings are less clear on a disaggregated basis

### GLEIF parent/child data

- GLEIF does **not** list a direct or ultimate legal-entity parent for Alphabet
- GLEIF instead shows **NO_KNOWN_PERSON** reporting exceptions for parent fields
- GLEIF lists direct children including **Google LLC** and several Google India entities

**Interpretation:** No separate legal-entity parent is shown in GLEIF; public ownership is transparent, but control is concentrated in the founders through voting rights.

#### False positive noted

A name-only OFAC hit exists for **“ALPHABET INTERNATIONAL DMCC”**, an unrelated sanctioned entity linked by OFAC to the **National Iranian Oil Company**. This is a **clear false positive** versus Alphabet Inc., but it is a useful reminder that name-screening controls should suppress or disambiguate similar-name hits.

### Indirect sanctions-related exposure

Alphabet’s 2025 10-K states it has **ongoing legal matters relating to Russia**, including disputes involving account terminations, **including those of sanctioned parties**. Alphabet states it does **not expect these matters to be material**.

**Assessment:** No evidence found of Alphabet Inc. being sanctioned, but there is limited indirect sanctions-touchpoint exposure through Russia-related legal disputes.

### PEP

- **Roger W. Ferguson Jr.** is the main public-source PEP-relevant name identified.
- Alphabet’s proxy states he served on the **Board of Governors of the U.S. Federal Reserve System** and was **Vice Chairman from 1999 to 2006**.
- Public open-source screening also returned him as a **PEP** result.

**Assessment:**

- **PEP exposure exists at board level**, but not in a way that suggests hidden political ownership or current state control.
- No clear public-source PEP indicators were identified for Page, Brin, or Pichai in this review.

## Litigation & Regulatory Actions

### 1) U.S. search antitrust case

Alphabet’s 2025 10-K states that:

- DOJ and state AGs sued Google over **Search and Search advertising**
- In **August 2024**, the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia ruled Google violated antitrust law
- Remedies proceedings could include material changes to products, distribution arrangements, and potentially structural relief

### 2) U.S. ad-tech monopolization case

The U.S. DOJ announced on **April 17, 2025** that it prevailed in *United States et al. v. Google*, with the court holding Google violated antitrust law by monopolizing **open-web digital advertising markets**.

### 3) Epic Games v. Google

Alphabet’s 2025 10-K states:

- A California jury found in **December 2023** that Google violated antitrust laws relating to Google Play
- A remedies order was issued in **October 2024**
- Alphabet is appealing

### 4) European Commission competition actions

Alphabet’s 2025 10-K summarizes major EC cases:

- **2017 shopping case:** €2.4bn fine; Alphabet states the ECJ upheld the decision in **September 2024**
- **2018 Android case:** original €4.3bn fine reduced to **€4.1bn** by the General Court; appeal remains pending
- **2019 AdSense case:** General Court annulled the €1.5bn fine in **September 2024**; EC appealed

### 5) Texas privacy settlement

Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton announced on **May 9, 2025** a **$1.375bn settlement** with Google related to alleged unlawful collection/tracking of Texans’ data involving:

- geolocation
- incognito searches
- biometric data

### 6) EU Digital Markets Act scrutiny

Reuters reported on **January 27, 2026** that the European Commission opened specification proceedings to guide Google’s compliance with the DMA regarding:

- access for rival online search engines
- access for AI providers to Google services and Gemini-related features
- access to search data and Android-related interfaces

**Assessment:** Alphabet’s litigation/regulatory profile is materially elevated and ongoing.

## Adverse Media

### Main adverse media themes identified

1. **Antitrust dominance / monopoly allegations**
   - Recurrent global coverage tied to DOJ cases, EU competition law, and remedies risk.
2. **Privacy and data collection**
   - Major scrutiny reinforced by the Texas settlement and broader public debate over geolocation, search, and biometric data handling.
3. **AI/platform governance**
   - Ongoing criticism from rivals and regulators over access to search data, Android ecosystems, and AI tooling.
4. **Russia-related disputes**
   - Limited but notable public disclosure around disputes involving terminated accounts, including sanctioned parties.

### Qualitative view

Adverse media is **persistent and high-volume**, but it is primarily tied to **market conduct, privacy, and regulation**, not classic financial-crime red flags such as bribery, money laundering, organized crime, or terrorism financing.

## Recommendation

## **Approve with EDD**

### Rationale

Alphabet is a highly transparent, regulated, publicly listed entity with clear legal existence, strong disclosure, and no direct sanctions hits identified in this OSINT review. However, the company’s **material antitrust, privacy, and regulatory exposure** is too significant for a plain-vanilla approval without enhanced controls.

### Conditions

1. **Verify the exact onboarding entity**
   - Confirm whether the counterparty is **Alphabet Inc.**, **Google LLC**, or another subsidiary.
   - Re-run KYC on the specific contracting entity if different from the parent.
2. **Treat Page and Brin as control persons**
   - Even if listed-company exemptions apply internally, their voting control should be documented.
3. **Apply PEP handling to Roger Ferguson Jr.**
   - Route through standard former-PEP governance under internal policy.
4. **Conduct licensed screening before final onboarding**
   - Run commercial sanctions/PEP/watchlist checks on:
     - Alphabet Inc.
     - contracting subsidiary
     - founders
     - senior management
     - board members
   - Public-source checks are helpful but incomplete.
5. **Set ongoing monitoring**
   - Monitor for developments in:
     - DOJ antitrust remedies
     - EU DMA proceedings
     - privacy enforcement
     - Russia/sanctions-related disputes
6. **Confirm geographic and product exposure**
   - Ensure the contemplated relationship does not involve sanctioned jurisdictions, restricted end-users, or high-risk products/services.

### Information gaps

- This was a **public-source OSINT review only**; no proprietary screening databases were used.
- I did **not exhaustively map all subsidiaries** or all global litigation matters.
- Ongoing appeals/remedies could materially change the risk picture after this memo date.
- If onboarding a subsidiary rather than Alphabet Inc., **entity-specific review is still required**.
